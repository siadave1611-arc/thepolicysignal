Week 12 · February 2025

Oil Tankers and Joint Debt: Two Faces of Sovereignty

How Washington's maritime enforcement and Brussels' financial gamble both reshape what sovereignty means in 2025.

Sovereignty Realism Liberalism Hard Power Unilateralism Multilateralism Interdependence
Article I

The U.S. Is Boarding Foreign Tankers — and Nobody's Stopping Them

Source: The New York Times

In Brief

The U.S. Coast Guard stopped and boarded a Panamanian-flagged tanker carrying Venezuelan oil — the second such action this month — escalating Trump's pressure campaign against the Maduro government.

This event puts two state actors in direct friction: the United States and Venezuela, with President Trump targeting Maduro's sovereignty through maritime enforcement. The U.S. approach here is unmistakably unilateral, aligning with John Mearsheimer's realist view that states prioritize national interests and power over international legal norms. By intercepting tankers without multilateral consent, Washington is projecting hard military power to enforce economic sanctions on its own terms.

The broader implication cuts deeper than one tanker. These boardings challenge Venezuela's external sovereignty — its ability to trade oil freely on the global market — and disrupt the financial flows that keep the Maduro regime operational. What we're really seeing is the U.S. asserting structural production power: controlling who gets to move resources through contested sea regions, and using its naval dominance to make that control stick.

What Happens Next

Venezuela will likely seek alternative shipping routes or adopt deceptive shipping practices to circumvent U.S. enforcement. Expect Caracas to deepen ties with non-Western partners — particularly China — to maintain external sovereignty and trade access, which will only sharpen the geopolitical divide over maritime control.

Article II

The EU Is Borrowing €90 Billion Together — and That Changes Everything

Source: Euronews

In Brief

The European Union will jointly borrow €90 billion to support Ukraine's defense against Russia. Funds will be disbursed gradually with conditions. Hungary, Slovakia, and the Czech Republic have opted out.

Where the U.S. acts alone, the EU acts collectively — and this €90 billion joint debt package is a textbook demonstration of pooled sovereignty. Member states are voluntarily sharing financial risk to support an ally, a move that only makes sense through a liberalist lens where multilateral cooperation and institutional trust outweigh narrow self-interest.

The deeper story is about complex interdependence. Ukraine's financial stability and sovereignty are now structurally tied to EU decision-making. This initiative shows how a state's economic power and structural financial power are shaped through global flows of aid and international institutions — not just military hardware. By stabilizing Ukraine's economy during wartime, the EU is reinforcing Ukraine's external sovereignty from the outside in.

What Happens Next

The opt-outs from Hungary, Slovakia, and the Czech Republic signal real internal friction. But the EU will likely push the package through — its liberalist architecture and commitment to regional unity have historically absorbed dissent. The bigger question is whether joint borrowing becomes a norm or stays an exception, because that answer reshapes European sovereignty permanently.

Two stories. Two opposite approaches to sovereignty. One state goes it alone with warships; another pools its money and its risk. Both are reshaping the rules — just from different directions.
